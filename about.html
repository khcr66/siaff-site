Seniors In Action Film Festival
About SIAFF

Born in a classroom.
Built by learners.

The Seniors In Action Film Festival didn't emerge from a funding body or an industry initiative. It grew out of a community of older adults who decided, one week at a time, to learn how to make films.

Where it started

In 2018, Thomas Yee — a filmmaker with over 35 years of professional experience in Canada — founded the filmmaking group within Angus Glen Older Adult Club (AGOAC) in Markham, Ontario. His premise was simple and radical: older adults with no prior filmmaking experience could learn the complete craft, from story concept to final distribution, if given the right environment and guidance.

The group met weekly. They learned by doing. Scripts were written, scenes were shot, edits were made. At the end of each year, every film made by every student was screened and critiqued together — the same honest, constructive peer review model used in serious film schools, applied to a community room in a suburban Ontario rec center.

"We were not trying to make professional filmmakers. We were trying to make people who had something to say, and the tools to say it."

How the festival was born

Filmmaking is a journey — and in this group, every step of that journey is visible. Some films were polished. Many were rough. Most were first attempts, carrying all the uncertainty and sincerity that first attempts carry. In 2024, the group decided that journey deserved a public moment. They formalized their annual review into a proper festival — with an independent jury panel, award categories, and a public screening.

The first SIAFF was not about technical achievement. It was about people who had committed to learning something difficult, and were willing to show what they had made along the way. That spirit has not changed. SIAFF has run annually since 2024, and in 2027 opens to senior filmmakers worldwide — because the belief that this journey deserves an audience was never just about Markham.

What makes us different

There are other film festivals that accept films by senior directors. SIAFF is the only one founded by people who were still learning the craft themselves. That origin isn't a footnote — it shapes everything about how we approach submissions, how we jury, and how we treat the filmmakers who trust us with their work.

SIAFF is simply what that same community shares with the world once a year, in public.

Independent and unaffiliated — by choice

SIAFF has no institutional sponsors and no ties to the film industry. We do not seek industry approval or insider recognition. There is no career ladder here, no networking agenda, no aspiration to be noticed by the people who run the mainstream festival circuit.

What we are is simpler—and, we believe, more honest: a home for senior filmmakers to share their passion for telling their own stories, in their own ways. We answer to our community — not to funders, not to industry gatekeepers, not to anyone whose endorsement we are trying to earn.

We do, however, actively welcome collaboration with other organizations that share our values — community-rooted, learner-centered, genuinely committed to giving senior voices a stage. If that describes your organization, we would be glad to hear from you.

Our position

We program short films by directors aged 65 and older. We prioritize films that highlight older adult perspectives, explore intergenerational relationships, or challenge what society thinks aging should look like. We judge on clarity of vision — not production budget. A first-time filmmaker with a smartphone and something specific to say belongs in our program as much as anyone.

Our values
▸ Honesty over flattery — in our jury, in our feedback, in our selections.
▸ Vision over polish — we judge what you set out to do, not what you spent.
▸ Community over prestige — not chasing industry approval.
▸ Access over exclusion — free to submit, always. Self-funded by choice.
▸ Collaboration over competition — we welcome like-minded organizations who share our values.
